Cristallin system & class



What's the difference between cristallin system and class ?

Crystal class is a fundamental concept in crystallography that refers to the symmetry of crystals. To understand this concept, one must first have an idea of ​​symmetry elements and point groups in crystallography.
In summary, crystal class is a classification of crystals based on their internal symmetry, which is essential for understanding and predicting the properties of crystalline materials.		 ≠ A crystal system is a classification of crystal structures based on the symmetries and lattice parameters of the crystals. In crystallography, crystals are classified into different crystal systems according to the lengths of the axes and the angles between them.
Each crystal system is defined by a specific symmetry group and lattice parameters that define how the atoms are arranged in space.


Element of Symmetry

Symmetry elements are operations that leave the crystal structure unchanged. These elements include:


Rotation axes: rotation about an axis by a specific angle (360°/n, where n is an integer);
Symmetry planes: reflection through a plane;
Inversion center: inversion through a central point;
Rotation-inversion axes: combination of a rotation followed by an inversion.


32 Cristallin classes


The 32 crystal classes can be organized into 7 crystal systems according to the types of symmetry they exhibit. The crystal systems are:


I.
Cubic system or isometric

The cubic class of minerals refers to a specific category within the isometric crystal system, characterized by three equal axes that intersect at right angles. Tetragonal system:

Orthorhombic system:

Importance of cristallin classes


Crystal classes are crucial for:

  • Mineral Identification: Symmetry helps to identify and classify minerals.
  • Physical Properties: The physical properties (optical, magnetic, etc.) of crystals depend on their symmetry.
  • Technological Applications: Understanding crystal classes helps to design materials with specific properties for various applications (electronics, optics, etc.).
